Case Study · Corpus Linguistics & Taxonomy

From Speech Analytics to Corporate Taxonomy

Bridging natural language variation with controlled vocabulary infrastructure at enterprise scale.
Speech Analytics Controlled Vocabulary Corporate Taxonomy ISO 25964 Semantic Relations 2M+ monthly interactions

1. Epistemological Positioning

Research Proposition: This applied research investigates how natural language variation in customer-service interactions creates semantic gaps in information retrieval systems. By analyzing speech analytics data and search logs, the project develops a methodological framework for transforming spontaneous speech patterns into governed corporate taxonomy, demonstrating how linguistic analysis can bridge human communication with computational systems.

In large-scale telecommunications customer service, the disconnect between how customers express problems and how systems understand those expressions creates operational inefficiencies and user frustration. The work develops from a localized controlled vocabulary to an enterprise-wide taxonomy, providing a replicable model for linguistic infrastructure development in corporate environments.

2. Research Context

The Empirical Challenge

  • Data Source: Speech analytics transcripts + CRM search logs from multinational telecom (2020–2025)
  • Scale: 30,000+ customer service agents, 2M+ monthly interactions
  • Problem: Semantic noise between customer expressions, agent searches, and system content
  • Linguistic Variance: Proliferation of non-standard technical jargon and regional idiomatic expressions

Methodological Approach

  • Speech Analytics Analysis: systematic examination of communication patterns
  • Semantic Gap Mapping: identifying divergences between expressed needs and system responses
  • Controlled Vocabulary Development: structured terminological systems with semantic relations
  • Taxonomic Expansion: scaling localized vocabulary to enterprise infrastructure

4. Transformation Workflow

Phase 1: Terminological Dispersion

Figure 2: Boolean proximity operators used for lexical pattern extraction — 41 variants identified

Methodological Analysis

  • Corpus Linguistics Methodology: Treating operational language as a corpus for systematic analysis, not error detection
  • Attested Usage as Evidence: 41 variants documented as legitimate data points, not mistakes
  • Friction Point Identification: The gap between customer expressions, agent searches, and system classifications reveals where intervention is needed

Critical Finding — The Agent's Dilemma: Agents developed their own intermediary vocabulary (12 search patterns) that neither matched customer expressions (41 variants) nor aligned with system classifications (3 terms). This created a "triple mismatch" requiring constant translation between layers — adding cognitive load and reducing efficiency.

Phase 2: From Description to Prescriptive Normalization

Figure 3: Negative constraints and procedural noise filtered during taxonomy development

Methodological Analysis

  • Usage-Driven Decision Making: Term selection emerged from systematic analysis of frequency, distribution, and operational relevance
  • Systemic Interoperability: Terms must function across search systems, knowledge bases, and agent training materials
  • Governance Artifact Creation: The "terms to avoid" list makes terminological decisions explicit and implementable
